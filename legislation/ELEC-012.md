# ELEC-012 - Campaign Finance Democratic Authority Implementation Act

This proposed enabling legislation depends on ratification of the [Campaign Finance Democratic Authority Amendment](ELEC-012-amendment.md), preferably through H.J.Res. 54, 119th Congress, with ARRP's recommended enforcement and federalism clarification, or a substantially similar amendment.

## A BILL

To implement constitutional authority to regulate campaign contributions, expenditures, artificial-entity political spending, disclosure, and public campaign-finance systems for federal elections, statewide elections, and statewide ballot measures.

## SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the **"Campaign Finance Democratic Authority Implementation Act."**

## SEC. 2. PURPOSES.

The purposes of this Act are to restore democratic authority over election money; ensure that voters can identify the true sources of large election-related spending; regulate artificial-entity political spending after ratification of the authorizing amendment; apply baseline standards to federal elections, statewide elections, and statewide ballot measures; reduce dependence on concentrated private wealth; preserve press freedom and individual political speech; and provide enforceable national standards for transparency, anti-evasion, and public campaign-finance systems.

## SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

For purposes of this Act:

**Artificial entity** means a corporation, limited liability company, partnership, labor organization, nonprofit organization, association, trust, political committee, or other legal entity that is not a natural person.

**Covered election** means a federal election, a covered statewide election, or a covered statewide ballot measure.

**Covered election spending** means a contribution, expenditure, independent expenditure, electioneering communication, coordinated communication, covered transfer, or other disbursement made to influence a covered election.

**Covered statewide ballot measure** means a proposed constitutional amendment, statute, referendum, initiative, recall, retention question, or other measure submitted to voters throughout a State.

**Covered statewide election** means an election for Governor, Lieutenant Governor, Attorney General, Secretary of State, Treasurer, Auditor, Comptroller, State supreme court or court of last resort, statewide elected regulatory office, United States Senator, presidential electors, or any other office elected by voters throughout a State.

**Covered transfer** means a transfer of funds or thing of value to another person or entity when the transferor knows, has reason to know, or designates that the funds may be used for covered election spending.

**Foreign-influenced entity** means an artificial entity in which a foreign national, foreign government, foreign political party, or entity organized under foreign law directly or indirectly owns or controls 20 percent or more of equity or voting power; has authority to appoint or direct any officer, director, general partner, trustee, or comparable decisionmaker; participates in any decision concerning covered election spending; directs, finances, or controls the covered election spending; or supplies funds used for a covered transfer.

**True source** means the natural person, artificial entity, or other source that provided funds for covered election spending before any pass-through, conduit, intermediary, or shell-entity transfer.

## SEC. 4. STATUTORY IMPLEMENTING RULES; LIMITED COMMISSION ADMINISTRATION.

The substantive rules in this Act are self-executing. No person, artificial entity, political committee, candidate, covered spender, or federal officer may treat the absence, delay, deadlock, withdrawal, or narrowing of Federal Election Commission regulations as suspending, limiting, or postponing any duty imposed by this Act.

Not later than 180 days after the effective date of this Act, the Commission shall publish forms, electronic filing specifications, database standards, advisory guidance, and ministerial inflation adjustments necessary to administer this Act. The Commission may issue gap-filling regulations only when consistent with the text, purposes, thresholds, deadlines, and anti-evasion rules enacted by Congress.

No Commission regulation, advisory opinion, enforcement manual, or failure to act may create an exemption, delay a deadline, narrow a definition, raise a disclosure threshold, authorize artificial-entity treasury spending, or otherwise defeat the statutory purpose unless expressly authorized by this Act.

If the Commission fails to publish required forms or electronic systems by the deadline, filings may be made in any reasonable written or electronic form containing the information required by this Act, and a covered filer acting in good faith shall not be penalized for failure to use an unavailable Commission form.

## SEC. 5. TRUE-SOURCE DISCLOSURE.

A person or artificial entity making more than $10,000 in aggregate covered election spending during an election cycle shall disclose the true source of the funds, the amount used, the covered election activity supported or opposed, and any intermediary through which funds passed.

Any source providing more than $1,000 in funds that are designated for, restricted to, solicited for, transferred for, or knowingly provided for covered election spending shall be identified by name, address, amount, date, and any known intermediary or conduit.

Reports shall be filed electronically not later than 48 hours after crossing a reporting threshold and not later than 24 hours after any additional covered election spending or covered transfer during the final 30 days before an election. Reports shall be made publicly searchable upon receipt.

The Commission may publish ministerial inflation adjustments for the dollar thresholds in this section, but may not raise a threshold by more than the cumulative change in the Consumer Price Index. The Commission may not delay reporting deadlines except for system outages, personal safety risks, constitutionally protected associational privacy, or other circumstances expressly authorized by this Act.

## SEC. 6. ARTIFICIAL-ENTITY ELECTION SPENDING.

After ratification of the authorizing constitutional amendment, an artificial entity may not make covered election spending from general treasury funds unless expressly permitted by this Act.

An artificial entity may make covered election spending through a separately segregated fund if the fund receives contributions only from natural persons who are United States citizens or lawful permanent residents, discloses all true sources required by this Act, and does not receive funds from a foreign-influenced entity, federal contractor account, shell entity, conduit, or other artificial-entity treasury.

This section does not prohibit bona fide press activity, ordinary commercial activity not undertaken to influence an election, internal communications to members, shareholders, or employees, or administration of a political committee funded by disclosed natural-person contributions.

An artificial entity that makes, authorizes, solicits, administers, or facilitates covered election spending shall obtain board, shareholder, member, or comparable governance approval as applicable to the entity, maintain records for not less than 6 years, and certify that the spending complies with this Act.

## SEC. 7. FOREIGN-INFLUENCED AND CONTRACTOR-LINKED SPENDING.

A foreign-influenced entity may not make covered election spending, provide funds for covered election spending, or use a domestic intermediary to evade this Act.

Federal contractors and entities seeking or holding substantial federal contracts, grants, licenses, or regulatory approvals may be subject to heightened disclosure, anti-coercion, and anti-evasion rules to prevent public power from being converted into election leverage.

## SEC. 8. PUBLIC CAMPAIGN-FINANCE SYSTEMS.

Congress may establish, fund, and administer public campaign-finance systems for covered elections, including small-donor matching, democracy vouchers, grants, tax credits, spending offsets, and other lawful systems designed to reduce dependence on concentrated private wealth.

Participation may be conditioned on reasonable contribution, expenditure, disclosure, debate, and accountability requirements consistent with the authorizing constitutional amendment.

## SEC. 9. ENFORCEMENT.

The Commission may investigate and impose civil penalties for violations of this Act. The Attorney General may bring civil actions to enforce this Act and may pursue criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations where otherwise authorized by law.

Any candidate, political committee, voter, donor, State attorney general, or organization directly affected by a violation may file an administrative complaint with the Commission. If the Commission fails to act within 90 days, dismisses a complaint because of deadlock, lacks a quorum, fails to publish required forms, or otherwise fails to enforce a self-executing duty, the complainant may bring a civil action in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia or in the district where the violation occurred.

The court may issue declaratory relief, injunctive relief, compliance orders, disclosure orders, civil penalties payable to the Treasury, fee awards, and any other relief necessary to enforce this Act. Exhaustion before the Commission is not required when delay would prevent voters from receiving required information before an election.

## SEC. 10. CONTINGENT INSTITUTIONAL SAFEGUARDS.

### (a) Trigger.

The safeguards in this section apply only upon a covered implementation failure. A covered implementation failure occurs when the Commission or another federal officer:

1. fails to publish required forms, electronic filing specifications, database standards, or ministerial guidance by a statutory deadline;
2. lacks a quorum, deadlocks, or otherwise fails to act on a complaint, filing, enforcement matter, advisory-opinion request, disclosure dispute, audit, or database duty within a statutory deadline or within 90 days if no shorter deadline applies;
3. issues, relies on, or refuses to withdraw an advisory opinion, enforcement policy, safe harbor, guidance document, or other agency position that narrows, delays, suspends, or contradicts a duty imposed by this Act;
4. fails to make required disclosure information public within the time required by this Act;
5. selectively enforces this Act in a manner not reasonably explained by statutory criteria, evidentiary differences, resource constraints, or ordinary enforcement priorities;
6. withholds, delays, conditions, impounds, or prevents the obligation or expenditure of funds appropriated to administer or enforce this Act;
7. removes, reassigns, disciplines, threatens, or materially burdens a commissioner, employee, contractor, filer, complainant, witness, or covered person because of lawful administration, compliance, disclosure, testimony, or enforcement under this Act; or
8. fails adequately to defend this Act, confesses error, enters a settlement, or takes a litigation position that would materially impair implementation without providing Congress and affected parties a reasonable opportunity to intervene.

### (b) Backup filing and public disclosure.

Upon a covered implementation failure affecting filing or publication, a filer may satisfy this Act by filing the required information with the Commission in any reasonable written or electronic form and by contemporaneously filing the same information with the Clerk of the United States District Court for the District of Columbia, the Government Accountability Office, and the committees of Congress with jurisdiction over federal elections or campaign finance.

The Clerk, GAO, or a congressional committee receiving a backup filing under this subsection may make the filing publicly available. Publication through a backup channel satisfies the public-disclosure requirement until the Commission restores ordinary publication.

### (c) Direct civil action and expedited review.

Upon a covered implementation failure, any candidate, political committee, voter, donor, State attorney general, filer, complainant, or organization directly affected by the failure may bring a civil action in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia or in the district where the violation, filing, or election activity occurred.

The court shall expedite any action brought under this section. Relief may include declaratory relief, temporary or permanent injunctive relief, disclosure orders, compliance schedules, appointment of a special master for disclosure administration, civil penalties payable to the Treasury, fee awards, and any other relief necessary to preserve voter access to required information before an election.

### (d) Intervention and defense of the Act.

Either House of Congress, a committee of Congress with jurisdiction over federal elections or campaign finance, the Bipartisan Legal Advisory Group or comparable House authority, the Senate Legal Counsel, a State attorney general, and any directly affected candidate, voter, filer, complainant, or organization may intervene as of right in any action challenging, enforcing, limiting, settling, or declining to defend this Act.

No settlement, confession of error, consent judgment, advisory opinion, nonenforcement position, or litigation position by the Department of Justice, the Commission, or any other executive officer shall bind Congress, State attorneys general, intervenors, or a reviewing court on the meaning, validity, or enforcement of this Act.

### (e) Funding and apportionment protection.

Amounts appropriated to administer or enforce this Act shall be apportioned, allotted, obligated, and made available in the ordinary course and may not be withheld, delayed, conditioned, impounded, or used to prevent implementation. Any apportionment, allotment, hiring restriction, procurement delay, database delay, or administrative condition that materially impairs implementation shall be treated as a covered implementation failure.

GAO may audit compliance with this subsection and report any withholding, delay, condition, impoundment, or implementation obstruction to Congress. A court may order funds made available, require filing-system or database performance, and enjoin any officer or employee from obstructing implementation.

### (f) Advisory opinions and safe harbors.

An advisory opinion, enforcement policy, guidance document, safe harbor, or failure to act by the Commission may not excuse noncompliance with a clear statutory duty. During a covered implementation failure, a regulated person may rely only on a safe harbor expressly stated in this Act or on a written judicial order.

### (g) Public interference notices.

Any commissioner, employee, contractor, filer, complainant, witness, or covered person who has substantial reason to believe that a federal officer or employee has obstructed, delayed, narrowed, retaliated against, or otherwise interfered with implementation of this Act may submit a confidential notice to the Inspector General of the Commission, GAO, and the committees of Congress with jurisdiction over federal elections or campaign finance. The receiving office shall protect lawful confidentiality, personal safety, classified information, grand-jury secrecy, and pending enforcement needs while ensuring that Congress receives sufficient information to assess implementation risk.

## SEC. 11. STATEWIDE ELECTIONS; STATE AUTHORITY PRESERVED.

The standards in this Act apply to covered statewide elections and covered statewide ballot measures to the same extent that they apply to federal elections.

As applied solely to covered statewide elections and covered statewide ballot measures, sections 5 through 10 shall take effect on the date that is 18 months after the effective date of this Act. A State may elect earlier application by statute, regulation, or written certification by the State's chief election officer or other officer designated by State law.

During the statewide-election transition period, each State may review its campaign-finance, corporate, ballot-measure, public-financing, disclosure, enforcement, and election-administration laws for consistency with this Act. The Commission, GAO, and the committees of Congress with jurisdiction over federal elections or campaign finance may publish nonbinding transition guidance, model crosswalks, and technical assistance materials for State review.

The transition period does not delay application of this Act to federal elections, excuse evasion through mixed-purpose spending, authorize foreign-influenced covered election spending, or prevent a State from enforcing any parallel or stronger State law.

Nothing in this Act requires a State or State officer to administer, investigate, enforce, publish, or defend this Act. A State may administer parallel or stronger campaign-finance rules under State law, and a State attorney general or State election authority may enforce this Act only as authorized by this Act or by State law.

Nothing in this Act preempts a State law governing campaign finance, corporate powers, disclosure, public financing, foreign-influenced spending, contractor-linked spending, ballot measures, or artificial-entity political activity unless compliance with both State law and this Act is impossible or the State law authorizes conduct prohibited by this Act.

For mixed-purpose communications, transfers, or spending that affect both a covered election and an election or ballot measure outside the scope of this Act, this Act applies to the portion, source, transfer, communication, or activity reasonably attributable to influencing the covered election. A covered person shall not evade this Act by combining covered election spending with noncovered election spending.

## SEC. 12. RULES OF CONSTRUCTION.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to abridge the freedom of the press; authorize viewpoint discrimination; suppress ordinary individual political speech; prevent reasonable protection of associational privacy; displace stronger State campaign-finance laws consistent with the Constitution; or require disclosure of small donors below thresholds established by section 5.

Disclosure duties under this Act shall be administered and construed to preserve narrow tailoring, protect against threats, harassment, reprisals, and comparable associational harms, and permit confidential filing, redaction, or delayed public disclosure when a court finds that such relief is required by the Constitution and does not defeat the Act's voter-information purpose.

Nothing in this Act authorizes a public campaign-finance mechanism that penalizes a nonparticipating speaker, awards public funds solely because of an opponent's spending, or otherwise burdens protected political expression except to the extent expressly authorized by the authorizing constitutional amendment.

Nothing in this Act shall take effect to the extent it depends on constitutional authority not yet supplied by ratification of the authorizing amendment.

## SEC. 13. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date the Archivist of the United States certifies ratification of H.J.Res. 54, 119th Congress, with ARRP's recommended enforcement and federalism clarification, or a substantially similar constitutional amendment authorizing Congress to regulate campaign contributions, expenditures, artificial-entity political spending, election-money disclosure, statewide elections, and statewide ballot measures.

## SEC. 14. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act, or the application of such provision to any person or circumstance, is held invalid, the remainder of this Act and the application of its provisions to any other person or circumstance shall not be affected.

## Budgetary Impact Statement

Budget authority is likely required for FEC administration and enforcement, public disclosure systems, database modernization, audits, public campaign-finance programs, and federal-state implementation support. No dollar estimate is assigned pending source-backed cost data.

## Drafting Notes

- This is an initial enabling-legislation draft, not a final statutory package.
- The Act is drafted to operate only after ratification of H.J.Res. 54 with ARRP's recommended enforcement and federalism clarification, or a substantially similar constitutional amendment.
- The Act now applies the same baseline standards to federal elections, covered statewide elections, and covered statewide ballot measures, while preserving stronger State rules and avoiding mandatory State administration of the federal Act.
- The statewide-election layer includes an 18-month transition period so States can review existing law against the new baseline; T2 should test whether that period should instead be keyed to election cycles or legislative sessions.
- If Congress proceeds under a narrower Democracy for All-style amendment, the artificial-entity and money-as-speech provisions should receive a conformity review.
- Section 10 is intentionally contingent: it preserves ordinary FEC administration unless defined implementation failures trigger backup filing, direct enforcement, intervention, funding-protection, and interference-reporting safeguards.
- T3 should decide whether this Act should be recast as conforming amendments to the Federal Election Campaign Act rather than a freestanding post-ratification statute, especially as to 52 U.S.C. §§ 30104, 30106, 30109, 30116, 30118, and 30121.
- Any direct-civil-action provision should be conformed to FECA's existing FEC complaint, four-vote, civil-enforcement, and citizen-suit structure before publication-ready reliance.
- Disclosure rules should be tested against *Buckley*, *McCutcheon*, and *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta* for true-source transparency, tailoring, harassment risk, and associational privacy.
- Public-financing provisions should avoid opponent-triggered matching-fund structures or other burdens on nonparticipating speakers identified in *Arizona Free Enterprise Club's Freedom Club PAC v. Bennett*.
- FEC structure and deadlock repair may require a separate companion proposal if ELEC-012 remains focused on substantive campaign-finance authority.
