# DOJ-009 — Senior Department of Justice Professional Qualification and Good Standing Act

## A BILL

To establish professional-qualification, good-standing, disciplinary-disclosure, continuing-notification, and temporary-delegation requirements for the Attorney General and selected senior Department of Justice legal officers, while preventing pending or politically motivated complaints from becoming automatic disqualification tools.

Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives of the United States of America in Congress assembled,

## SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the **“Senior Department of Justice Professional Qualification and Good Standing Act.”**

## SEC. 2. PURPOSES.

The purposes of this Act are to—

1. preserve the professional legitimacy of the Department of Justice;
2. ensure that senior Department legal officers possess baseline professional qualifications to supervise the legal work of the United States;
3. require admission to practice law and good standing for covered senior legal offices;
4. require disclosure of bar memberships, disciplinary history, and pending formal disciplinary charges;
5. require continuing notice of final professional discipline while serving;
6. establish temporary delegation procedures where a covered officer loses professional good standing or receives final discipline materially inconsistent with senior Department legal service;
7. preserve presidential nomination authority and Senate advice-and-consent authority subject to statutory qualifications for offices Congress creates; and
8. prevent pending, unadjudicated, frivolous, or politically motivated ethics complaints from automatically disqualifying nominees or officers.

## SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

### (a) Covered office.

The term **“covered office”** means—

1. Attorney General;
2. Acting Attorney General;
3. Deputy Attorney General;
4. Acting Deputy Attorney General;
5. Associate Attorney General;
6. Acting Associate Attorney General;
7. Solicitor General;
8. Acting Solicitor General;
9. Assistant Attorney General;
10. Acting Assistant Attorney General; and
11. any other Department of Justice office designated by the Attorney General by regulation as a senior legal-supervisory office for purposes of this Act.

### (b) Covered officer.

The term **“covered officer”** means any person nominated, appointed, designated, or serving in a covered office.

### (c) United States jurisdiction.

The term **“United States jurisdiction”** means any State, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, Guam, the Northern Mariana Islands, the Virgin Islands, American Samoa, any federally recognized tribal court jurisdiction that licenses attorneys, or any federal court that admits attorneys to practice.

### (d) Good standing.

The term **“good standing”** means that the covered officer is currently admitted to practice law in at least one United States jurisdiction and is not suspended, disbarred, resigned in lieu of discipline, administratively ineligible for disciplinary reasons, or otherwise finally determined by a licensing authority to be ineligible to practice law in that jurisdiction.

### (e) Final professional discipline.

The term **“final professional discipline”** means a final, non-interlocutory order, judgment, decision, or disposition by a court, bar authority, disciplinary authority, or licensing authority imposing disbarment, suspension, public censure, public reprimand, resignation in lieu of discipline, reciprocal discipline, or comparable public professional discipline.

### (f) Pending formal disciplinary charge.

The term **“pending formal disciplinary charge”** means a formally filed public disciplinary complaint, charge, petition, or equivalent adjudicatory proceeding initiated by a court, bar authority, disciplinary authority, or licensing authority after preliminary screening or investigation.

The term does not include a grievance, informal complaint, request for investigation, private complaint, media allegation, congressional complaint, political complaint, or preliminary inquiry that has not resulted in a formally filed disciplinary charge by an authorized disciplinary body.

### (g) Materially inconsistent discipline.

The term **“materially inconsistent discipline”** means final professional discipline that, considering the nature of the covered office, materially bears on the covered officer's ability to exercise legal supervision, prosecutorial judgment, candor to tribunals, professional independence, fiduciary duties, confidentiality obligations, or ethical duties owed to the United States.

## SEC. 4. PROFESSIONAL QUALIFICATION REQUIREMENT.

### (a) Requirement.

A person may not be nominated, appointed, designated, or continue serving in a covered office unless the person is admitted to practice law and in good standing in at least one United States jurisdiction.

### (b) Applicability to acting service.

The requirement in subsection (a) applies to any acting designation under the Federal Vacancies Reform Act of 1998, 5 U.S.C. §§ 3345 through 3349d, or any other statute authorizing acting service in a covered office.

### (c) No effect on non-legal offices.

This Act does not impose a bar-admission requirement on Department of Justice offices that are not covered offices.

## SEC. 5. PRE-NOMINATION, APPOINTMENT, AND DESIGNATION DISCLOSURE.

### (a) Disclosure required.

Before nomination, appointment, or acting designation to a covered office, the covered officer shall provide to the President, the Department of Justice Designated Agency Ethics Official, and, for Senate-confirmed offices, the Senate Judiciary Committee—

1. all current and former bar memberships;
2. all jurisdictions in which the officer is currently admitted to practice law;
3. current good-standing status in each jurisdiction where admitted;
4. all final professional discipline;
5. any resignation in lieu of discipline;
6. any reciprocal discipline;
7. any pending formal disciplinary charge;
8. any administrative ineligibility based on professional-discipline reasons; and
9. any material limitation on the officer's eligibility to practice law.

### (b) Certification.

The covered officer shall certify under penalty of perjury that the disclosure is true, complete, and correct to the best of the officer's knowledge after reasonable inquiry.

### (c) Supplementation.

The covered officer shall supplement the disclosure promptly if information materially changes before confirmation, appointment, or designation.

## SEC. 6. CONTINUING DUTY TO NOTIFY.

### (a) Notice required.

A covered officer serving in a covered office shall notify the President, the Deputy Attorney General or next highest non-conflicted Department official, the Department of Justice Inspector General, the Office of Professional Responsibility, the Designated Agency Ethics Official, the Office of Government Ethics, and, for Senate-confirmed offices, the Senate Judiciary Committee, not later than 7 days after—

1. loss of good standing in any jurisdiction;
2. imposition of final professional discipline;
3. resignation in lieu of discipline;
4. initiation of a pending formal disciplinary charge;
5. reciprocal discipline;
6. suspension, disbarment, or administrative ineligibility for disciplinary reasons; or
7. any material limitation on eligibility to practice law.

### (b) Contents.

The notice shall identify the jurisdiction, disciplinary body, nature of the action, effective date, current procedural posture, and any limitation on the covered officer's ability to practice law.

### (c) Public reporting.

For Senate-confirmed covered offices, the Department shall publish a public notice of final professional discipline, suspension, disbarment, resignation in lieu of discipline, or loss of all good-standing status, except that information may be redacted to protect privacy, national security, law-enforcement sensitivity, or legally protected information.

## SEC. 7. EFFECT OF PENDING COMPLAINTS AND PENDING FORMAL CHARGES.

### (a) No automatic disqualification.

A pending complaint, grievance, allegation, request for investigation, media allegation, congressional complaint, political complaint, or pending formal disciplinary charge shall not automatically disqualify a person from nomination, appointment, designation, or service in a covered office.

### (b) Disclosure and evaluation.

A pending formal disciplinary charge shall be disclosed and may be considered by the President, the Senate, the Department of Justice, the Office of Government Ethics, and appropriate ethics officials in evaluating professional fitness, conflicts, or temporary recusal needs.

### (c) Protection against weaponization.

No legal consequence under this Act may be based solely on a complaint or allegation that has not resulted in a pending formal disciplinary charge or final professional discipline.

## SEC. 8. TEMPORARY DELEGATION UPON LOSS OF GOOD STANDING OR FINAL DISCIPLINE.

### (a) Automatic temporary delegation.

If a covered officer loses good standing in all United States jurisdictions in which the officer is admitted, is suspended or disbarred in all such jurisdictions, resigns in lieu of discipline, or receives final professional discipline materially inconsistent with service in the covered office, the legal-supervisory duties of that office shall temporarily devolve to the next eligible covered officer or other officer authorized by law.

### (b) Scope.

Temporary delegation under this section applies to legal-supervisory duties, litigation authority, prosecutorial supervision, professional-responsibility supervision, approval of legal positions, and other duties materially requiring professional legal judgment.

### (c) Presidential and Senate authority preserved.

Nothing in this section prevents the President from nominating, appointing, or designating an eligible replacement, or prevents the Senate from confirming an eligible nominee.

### (d) Restoration.

A covered officer whose duties are temporarily delegated may resume those duties only after regaining good standing in at least one United States jurisdiction and after the Designated Agency Ethics Official, in consultation with the Office of Professional Responsibility and the Office of Government Ethics, determines that no unresolved final professional discipline remains materially inconsistent with service.

### (e) Written determination.

Any restoration determination shall be transmitted to the President, the Department of Justice Inspector General, the Office of Professional Responsibility, the Office of Government Ethics, and, for Senate-confirmed offices, the Senate Judiciary Committee.

## SEC. 9. MATERIAL-INCONSISTENCY REVIEW.

### (a) Review required.

If a covered officer receives final professional discipline other than disbarment, suspension, or resignation in lieu of discipline, the Designated Agency Ethics Official, in consultation with the Office of Professional Responsibility and the Office of Government Ethics, shall determine whether the discipline is materially inconsistent with service in the covered office.

### (b) Factors.

The determination shall consider—

1. the nature and seriousness of the misconduct;
2. whether the misconduct involved dishonesty, fraud, deceit, misrepresentation, obstruction, abuse of legal process, misuse of client information, breach of fiduciary duty, or lack of candor to a tribunal;
3. whether the misconduct relates to public office, government service, prosecutorial judgment, law-enforcement authority, professional supervision, or litigation conduct;
4. whether the discipline is final;
5. whether the officer remains in good standing in at least one United States jurisdiction;
6. whether temporary recusal, screening, or delegation can adequately protect the Department's interests; and
7. the public interest in the Department's integrity and professional independence.

### (c) Written determination.

The determination shall be written and transmitted to the President, the Department of Justice Inspector General, the Office of Professional Responsibility, the Office of Government Ethics, and, for Senate-confirmed offices, the Senate Judiciary Committee.

## SEC. 10. RECUSAL, SCREENING, AND LIMITED DELEGATION.

If final professional discipline or a pending formal disciplinary charge presents a specific conflict, appearance concern, or professional-responsibility issue short of automatic temporary delegation, the covered officer shall consult with the Designated Agency Ethics Official and Office of Professional Responsibility regarding recusal, screening, limited delegation, or other protective measures.

## SEC. 11. OFFICE OF PROFESSIONAL RESPONSIBILITY AND INSPECTOR GENERAL REVIEW.

### (a) OPR review.

The Office of Professional Responsibility shall review any final professional discipline, loss of good standing, or pending formal disciplinary charge involving a covered officer where the matter may affect the officer's legal-supervisory duties.

### (b) Inspector General review.

The Department of Justice Inspector General may review Department compliance with this Act, including disclosure, notification, delegation, and restoration procedures.

### (c) Reports.

The Inspector General may report findings and recommendations to the Attorney General, the President, Congress, or the public, consistent with law and appropriate protections for confidential or protected information.

## SEC. 12. REGULATIONS.

Not later than 180 days after enactment, the Attorney General shall promulgate regulations implementing this Act, including procedures for disclosure, verification of good standing, notice, temporary delegation, restoration, recusal, screening, and material-inconsistency review.

If the Attorney General is unavailable, conflicted, ineligible, or subject to review under this Act, the Deputy Attorney General or next eligible non-conflicted covered officer shall promulgate the regulations.

## SEC. 13. RULES OF CONSTRUCTION.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to—

1. require disqualification based solely on a pending complaint, grievance, allegation, request for investigation, media allegation, congressional complaint, political complaint, or pending formal disciplinary charge;
2. limit the President's nomination authority except by establishing statutory qualifications for offices created by law;
3. limit the Senate's advice-and-consent authority;
4. create a private right of action;
5. authorize collateral attack on otherwise valid Department actions solely because of a covered officer's later-discovered disclosure violation, unless otherwise provided by law;
6. limit any stricter ethics, recusal, disciplinary, or professional-responsibility requirement otherwise applicable to a covered officer; or
7. prevent Congress from requesting or considering information about pending complaints, professional fitness, or disciplinary matters during confirmation or oversight.

## SEC. 14. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision or application of this Act is held invalid, the remainder shall not be affected.

## Drafting Notes

- This is a first working draft.
- The draft deliberately distinguishes pending complaints and pending formal charges from final professional discipline.
- Pending complaints are not automatic disqualifiers.
- Pending formal disciplinary charges trigger disclosure and may support ethics review, recusal, screening, or Senate evaluation, but not automatic disqualification.
- Automatic temporary delegation is reserved for loss of good standing in all admitted jurisdictions, suspension or disbarment in all such jurisdictions, resignation in lieu of discipline, or final professional discipline materially inconsistent with senior DOJ legal service.
- Appointments Clause and separation-of-powers review remains necessary.
- Source development should review statutory qualifications for other federal offices, DOJ office statutes, OPR rules, OIG authority, OGE rules, Senate confirmation practice, and professional licensing law.
