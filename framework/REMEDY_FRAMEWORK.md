# Remedy Framework

| Level | Remedy Category | Typical Examples | Default Emphasis | Framework Rule |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Executive action | Directives, executive-branch practice, voluntary compliance measures | High where lawful and durable | Consider first, but do not rely on voluntary restraint where enforceable safeguards are needed. |
| 2 | Internal institutional reform | Ethics rules, succession practices, professional standards, organizational safeguards | High | Prefer where institutions can implement the remedy independently and durably. |
| 3 | Agency rule or regulation | Binding procedures, disclosure requirements, recusal rules, internal review | High | Evaluate statutory authority, durability, and vulnerability to repeal. |
| 4 | Ordinary legislation | Substantive limits, reporting duties, timelines, funding rules, enforcement authority | High | Develop fully where Congress can lawfully solve the problem. |
| 5 | Procedural reform | Mandatory votes, deadlines, disclosure, confirmation procedures, automatic termination | High | Use to reduce delay, evasion, and dependence on discretionary enforcement. |
| 6 | Enforcement or judicial review | Standing, expedited review, sanctions, independent enforcement, remedies | High | A right or limit is inadequate if no timely and reliable enforcement mechanism exists. |
| 7 | Automatic stabilizer or trigger | Expiration of acting authority, caretaker succession, compulsory review, temporary contraction of authority | Variable; high when persistent failure is central | Cross-reference the shared trigger regime rather than duplicating the substantive defect. |
| 8 | Constitutional amendment | Changes to tenure, appointment authority, succession, constructive removal, early elections | Lower by default; high when necessary | Always include when serious, but generally prefer adequate non-amendment solutions because Article V is arduous and slow. |

## Trigger Stages

| Trigger Stage | Illustrative Consequence | Typical Threshold | Safeguard | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Notice | Mandatory public reporting and written justification | Initial persistent failure | Objective reporting rule | Creates a formal record and prevents silent continuation. |
| Deadline | Fixed deadline for nomination, compliance, response, or corrective action | Defined time period | Clear statutory clock | Should account for emergencies and good-faith delay. |
| Compulsory action | Mandatory legislative or institutional vote | Missed deadline or specified failure | Recorded vote and transparent findings | Reduces strategic inaction. |
| Authority expiration | Automatic expiration of acting or emergency authority | Continued failure after deadline | Anti-evasion rule | Delegation should not recreate the expired authority. |
| Caretaker succession | Temporary authority passes to a confirmed or constitutionally eligible official | Extended vacancy or incapacity | Limited mandate and duration | Caretaker should preserve continuity without major policy innovation. |
| Expedited review | Automatic or accelerated judicial review | Disputed trigger or noncompliance | Due process and appeal | Prevents delay from defeating the remedy. |
| Temporary contraction | Specified discretionary authority is limited, suspended, or reassigned | Serious persistent failure | Proportionality and constitutional authority | Use only where lesser consequences are inadequate. |
| Capacity review | Formal institutional-capacity review | Multiple cumulative failures | Heightened evidentiary standard | Assesses cumulative constitutional nonfunction. |
| Constitutional mechanism | Constructive removal, temporary transfer, or early election | Extraordinary cumulative failure | Supermajority, due process, judicial review | Requires constitutional amendment where present constitutional structure does not authorize it. |
