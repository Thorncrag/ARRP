# HOR-036 Priority Case Review

This internal source-development memorandum reviews the six cases attached to HOR-036, which asks whether consequential executive action can operate long enough to cause difficult-to-repair harm because threshold doctrines prevent merits review or interim relief is unavailable. The review tests those cases as possible evidence; it does not assume that challenged executive action was unlawful or that every threshold dismissal identifies a defect.

## Finding

The six-case sample does not presently establish a recurring, cross-subject institutional failure warranting a general proposal. It contains useful illustrations of standing, ripeness, waiver, statutory review bars, and preliminary-relief limits, but it does not yet demonstrate the required sequence: materially consequential action, no timely merits route, concrete difficult-to-repair harm before review, and no adequate subject-specific remedy.

## Case Review

- ***Gwich'in Steering Committee v. Bernhardt*.** The district court [denied preliminary relief](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/alaska/akdce/3%3A2020cv00204/65089/69/) before an Arctic Refuge lease sale because the record of decision authorized no immediate on-the-ground activity and the court expected to decide the merits before immediate environmental injury. The order acknowledged possible longer-term injury and bureaucratic momentum, but it did not find immediate physical harm or foreclose later merits review. The episode is a useful comparator, not proof of HOR-036's complete predicate.

- ***California v. Wheeler*, D.C. Cir. No. 20-1357.** The D.C. Circuit denied a stay or summary vacatur of an EPA methane-rule rollback but expedited the merits schedule. Congress later [disapproved the rule under the Congressional Review Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/senate-joint-resolution/14). The temporary operation of the rule warrants policy-specific review, but this record does not establish that Article III or remedial doctrine prevented a merits route or caused identified irreparable harm that ordinary political and legal processes could not correct.

- ***Ramos v. Wolf*.** A Ninth Circuit panel held the statutory challenge to Temporary Protected Status terminations unreviewable under 8 U.S.C. § 1254a(b)(5)(A) while rejecting the equal-protection claim. The panel opinion was later [vacated for rehearing en banc](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/18-16981/18-16981-2020-09-14.html), and the litigation became moot after policy changes. The statutory review bar is a serious subject-specific question, but the unstable disposition and immigration-specific statute make RIGHTS-002 and REG-003 better present homes than a general judicial framework.

- ***Wild Virginia v. Council on Environmental Quality*.** The Fourth Circuit [affirmed dismissal](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca4/21-1839/21-1839-2022-12-22.html) of a facial challenge to the 2020 NEPA rules because several claims had become moot after rule revisions and the remainder depended on future agency applications. It expressly left application-specific challenges available. The decision shows the cost of project-by-project review, but not completed harm that no later challenge could remedy.

- ***California v. Wheeler*, N.D. Cal. No. 20-cv-03005.** The district court [denied preliminary relief](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/california/candce/3%3A2020cv03005/358957/171/) against the Navigable Waters Protection Rule after assessing likelihood of success and the other interim-relief factors. Later proceedings became moot after remand, and a separate district court vacated the rule. The episode should not be coded as final judicial approval of the rule and does not independently establish an irreparable review gap.

- ***Northern Alaska Environmental Center v. Department of the Interior*.** The Ninth Circuit [affirmed the challenged lease-sale analysis](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/19-35008/19-35008-2020-07-09.html) on the merits while treating one contention as waived. Because executive authority was not left wholly untested, this is principally a REG-006 lawful-agency-action comparator rather than HOR-036 evidence.

## Adversarial Check

The strongest contrary argument is that aggregate delay and repeated inability to obtain facial or interim review can itself impose irreversible environmental, immigration, or regulatory harms even if a later application-specific suit remains theoretically available. That concern is plausible, but this sample does not quantify the harm, show that it occurred before review, or establish that a subject-specific cause of action or interim-relief rule would be inadequate. A general remedy could also erode Article III limits, invite advisory litigation, and duplicate proposal-specific review provisions.

## Recommendation

Do not develop a general HOR-036 proposal from this six-case sample. At formal admission review, presumptively route the cases to their subject-specific homes and retire or merge HOR-036 unless additional controlling records demonstrate a recurring complete predicate across subjects. If the candidate remains open, the next source search should target cases with documented completed harm before merits review—not additional threshold dismissals standing alone.
