# WAR-001 Source Development — Manifestations and Examples

These notes collect concrete examples supporting the collapsed A-03 concern. They are working notes for later refinement of the issue file and legislative text.

## 1. Ambiguous threshold for “hostilities” — Libya, 2011

**Example.** During the 2011 Libya intervention, the executive branch argued that continued U.S. participation did not constitute “hostilities” under the War Powers Resolution, despite U.S. air, refueling, surveillance, and support roles and congressional objections.

**Manifestation.** The statutory term “hostilities” permitted executive interpretation narrow enough to avoid the 60-day authorization/termination consequence.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports definitions of “hostilities,” “covered military operation,” “offensive hostilities,” and the anti-relabeling rule.

## 2. Termination-clock weakness — Libya, 2011

**Example.** The 60-day deadline under the War Powers Resolution passed during the Libya operation without specific congressional authorization, while the executive maintained that the operation did not trigger the Resolution’s termination requirement.

**Manifestation.** The clock can be evaded if the predicate term “hostilities” is disputed, if termination duties are weak, or if Congress lacks effective enforcement tools.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports automatic termination, withdrawal-plan, no-slow-walking, funding-cutoff, and legal-basis certification provisions.

## 3. Concurrent-resolution / legislative-veto problem — INS v. Chadha

**Example.** INS v. Chadha held that Congress cannot impose binding legal consequences through a legislative veto outside bicameralism and presentment. The War Powers Resolution’s concurrent-resolution termination mechanism is therefore constitutionally vulnerable as a binding legal device.

**Manifestation.** A statutory framework that relies on concurrent resolutions can produce political statements without enforceable legal effect.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports presentment-compliant termination, authorization, limitation, and funding mechanisms, and the rule against treating concurrent or simple resolutions as binding legal consequences.

## 4. Stale and overbroad AUMF reliance — 2001 AUMF

**Example.** The 2001 AUMF has been relied on across many years, countries, groups, and theaters beyond the immediate Afghanistan/al-Qaeda context. The phrase “associated forces” does not appear in the statutory text but has become central to executive-branch practice.

**Manifestation.** A short authorization can become a durable platform for geographically and organizationally expanding military operations through interpretation rather than renewed congressional authorization.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports AUMF specificity, associated-force identification, geographic limits, mission limits, sunsets, renewal requirements, and no geographic or mission drift.

## 5. Repeal efforts for obsolete Iraq authorizations — 1991 and 2002 Iraq AUMFs

**Example.** Repeated bipartisan efforts have sought repeal of the 1991 and 2002 Iraq AUMFs, including Senate passage of repeal legislation in 2023. These efforts reflect concern that old authorizations can remain on the books after their original factual and strategic predicates have changed.

**Manifestation.** Obsolete authorizations may create latent executive authority or be cited in unexpected contexts unless Congress affirmatively sunsets, repeals, or narrows them.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports default AUMF sunsets, renewal requirements, existing-AUMF reliance reporting, and limits on construing authorizations beyond enemy, geography, mission, and temporal scope.

## 6. Executive legal-interpretation control — Libya and AUMF practice

**Example.** In Libya, executive legal analysis drove the conclusion that the operation did not constitute “hostilities” requiring termination. In AUMF practice, executive interpretations of covered groups, associated forces, and geographic scope have shaped the practical reach of statutory authority.

**Manifestation.** If the executive controls the operative legal interpretation, Congress may not know the full legal theory being used to initiate, continue, or expand military action.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports material legal-interpretation access, no secret expansion, and legal-basis certification.

## 7. Insufficient congressional information before commitment

**Example.** War-powers debates frequently arise after force is already initiated or after the executive branch has framed the factual and legal basis. Libya, Syria, Iran-related disputes, and AUMF renewal/repeal debates illustrate recurring congressional complaints that information is incomplete, classified, late, or framed through executive legal conclusions.

**Manifestation.** Congress may be asked to authorize, tolerate, fund, or terminate force without adequate information about legal basis, intelligence, mission objectives, costs, escalation risk, civilian-harm risk, alternatives, allied participation, and exit criteria.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports pre-authorization information requirements and classified annex procedures.

## 8. Mission expansion beyond authorization — 2001 AUMF and post-authorization practice

**Example.** The 2001 AUMF has been applied to groups and theaters far removed from the immediate perpetrators and harborers of the September 11 attacks. This illustrates how authorizations can be operationally extended through associated-force, successor-force, geography, and mission interpretations.

**Manifestation.** An authorization for one enemy, geography, or objective can become a basis for a different conflict unless material mission expansion requires renewed congressional authorization.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports material mission expansion definitions, notice, and reauthorization requirements.

## 9. Appropriations as implied authorization

**Example.** Congress may continue funding military personnel, logistics, equipment, intelligence, or withdrawal-related needs while disagreeing with the legality or wisdom of the operation. Executives may then argue that continued funding reflects congressional acquiescence or implied authorization.

**Manifestation.** Support-the-troops funding can be converted into practical authorization unless Congress must speak expressly.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports express-authorization language requirements and a rule that troop support, evacuation, medical care, force protection, intelligence, and withdrawal funding are not authorization for hostilities.

## 10. Partisan or institutional oversight collapse

**Example.** War-powers enforcement often depends on whether congressional majorities are willing to confront a President, whether leadership allows votes, whether committees act, and whether members are willing to risk political responsibility for limiting or ending military action.

**Manifestation.** Even when members object, oversight may fail if there is no mandatory vote, minority-triggered process, reporting obligation, or funding consequence.

**Relevance to bill.** Supports expedited procedures, minority-triggered review, mandatory reporting, funding consequences, and presentment-compliant votes.

## Source-development priorities

Further review should prioritize:

1. War Powers Resolution text and legislative history;
2. INS v. Chadha and post-Chadha treatment of legislative vetoes;
3. Libya 2011 executive legal materials, congressional hearings, and reports;
4. 2001 AUMF text, executive interpretations, associated-force practice, and CRS country-use reports;
5. 1991 and 2002 Iraq AUMF repeal efforts;
6. National Security Powers Act of 2021 and House companion proposals;
7. No More Presidential Wars Act and authorization-first proposals;
8. OLC opinions on Kosovo, Libya, Syria, and other military operations;
9. CRS reports on war-powers practice, AUMFs, and presidential reporting;
10. funding-cutoff and appropriations precedents.
