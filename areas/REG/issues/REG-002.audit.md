## Audit History

### 2026-07-21 — Horizon integration and scope Change Audit

**Trigger:** The project adjudicated HOR-025 and directed its merger-enforcement record into REG-002 while retitling and narrowing REG-002 to prevent institution-by-institution scope creep.

**Finding:** REG-002 now owns executive influence over particular noncriminal regulatory investigations, merger reviews, settlements, and enforcement decisions. It does not treat lawful priority setting, ordinary disagreement with career recommendations, criminal prosecution, rulemaking, administrative adjudication, agency-wide nullification, or regulatory retaliation as the same defect. The common institutional concern is whether case-specific intervention, contacts, conflicts, professional analysis, and reasons are adequately recorded and reviewable.

**Evidence treatment:** The HPE–Juniper personnel and settlement record, the *Faust* complaint, and the reported Paramount shareholder derivative action are retained as allegations and process evidence rather than corruption findings. The separate state antitrust challenge is excluded as an ordinary merits action unless it later produces evidence bearing on executive intervention. The 2019 automaker investigation supplies a neutral comparator because the DOJ Inspector General reviewed political-influence allegations and found insufficient evidence.

**Remedy fit:** The preliminary direction regulates records, contacts, conflicts, preservation, oversight access, and retaliation reporting without giving career personnel a veto or authorizing merits substitution. A statutory vehicle remains pending review of existing authorities and agency-specific confidentiality constraints.

**Score and run effect:** No score-bearing T-audit occurred. Score and Runs remain zero. The proposal remains In development.

**Next review need:** Map existing executive-contact policies, ethics and recusal rules, inspector-general jurisdiction, whistleblower protection, the Tunney Act, merger-review confidentiality, and comparable safeguards at DOJ, FTC, FCC, EPA, and other representative agencies. Determine whether one government-wide vehicle can operate without displacing agency-specific law.
