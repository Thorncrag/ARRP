# ELEC-013 Presidential Debate and Ballot Access Explanatory Checklist

> **Living research record:** This explainer supports [ELEC-013 issue #41](https://github.com/Thorncrag/ARRP/issues/41) and is reviewed through that proposal's issue-level monitoring.

Working source-development explainer for [ELEC-013](../issues/ELEC-013.md). This is not legal advice and does not replace current state law, official election-office guidance, campaign counsel, FEC regulations, advisory opinions, court orders, or debate-sponsor criteria. Exact signature counts, filing windows, elector forms, certification dates, and challenge procedures must be verified against current-cycle official sources before use.

## Core Finding

Presidential debate access and presidential ballot access are procedurally linked. A debate sponsor can use objective participation criteria under 11 C.F.R. 110.13, and modern criteria have often combined polling thresholds with proof that a candidate is on the ballot in enough states to have a path to 270 electoral votes. That means a debate rule can turn fifty-one separate state and District of Columbia ballot-access systems into a national gatekeeping threshold.

The practical lesson is visible in the 2024 RFK Jr. debate dispute: CNN reportedly required ballot access in states totaling at least 270 electoral votes and 15 percent support in four reliable national polls, while reported uncertainty over submitted-versus-certified ballot access affected whether states counted toward the threshold. That same structure explains why ELEC-013 should treat debate transparency, polling access, ballot-access timing, and state certification proof as one connected candidate-access problem.

## Track 0: National Campaign Setup

1. Confirm constitutional eligibility for president and vice president, including age, citizenship, residence, and any disability or disqualification issues.
2. Decide the ballot route before organizing field operations: major-party nomination, recognized minor-party nomination, new-party petition, independent petition, or write-in fallback.
3. Establish testing-the-waters controls before crossing the federal candidate threshold.
4. Once the campaign raises or spends more than $5,000, complete federal candidate registration and reporting steps, including a principal campaign committee, treasurer, tax ID, bank account, recordkeeping, and FEC reporting calendar.
5. Build a national ballot-access matrix with one row per state and the District of Columbia.
6. Assign a responsible owner for each jurisdiction, plus a backup owner for deadline, signature, elector, fee, and challenge tracking.
7. Create a master evidence file that can support both ballot certification and debate qualification: FEC filings, state filings, elector slates, receipts, signature counts, validation reports, certification letters, court orders, and correspondence.

## Track 1: Getting Into Presidential Debates

1. Identify the debate sponsor and legal posture: FEC-regulated staging organization, broadcaster, press entity, university host, public-broadcasting partner, nonprofit sponsor, public-benefit recipient, or private campaign-negotiated event.
2. Collect the published candidate-selection criteria before campaign planning relies on the debate: ballot-access cutoff, polling threshold, poll list, date range, methodology requirements, filing deadline, invitation deadline, certification proof, party-nominee treatment, and appeal process.
3. Confirm that the criteria are pre-established, objective, and not based solely on nomination by a particular political party for a general-election debate.
4. Build the ballot-access proof packet. For each counted jurisdiction, distinguish between claimed access, submitted paperwork, accepted paperwork, pending verification, final certification, court-ordered access, and official ballot listing.
5. Translate ballot access into Electoral College math. Debate criteria built around 270 electoral votes require a live tally based on officially countable states, not merely campaign claims.
6. Build the polling proof packet. Confirm the candidate is included by name in qualifying polls, that pollsters meet the sponsor's criteria, that field dates fall within the window, and that the candidate crosses the numerical threshold under the sponsor's rounding and publication rules.
7. Preserve evidence of alternate support if reform proposals later require or permit alternative qualification paths: petition signatures, small donors, fundraising breadth, prior vote share, party nominations, state-certified ballot access, or credible regional support.
8. Submit a debate-qualification packet before the deadline with a cover letter, state-by-state ballot evidence, polling evidence, FEC filing evidence, candidate eligibility evidence, and contact information for rapid cure.
9. Request a written decision from the sponsor that identifies every accepted and rejected state, poll, or document.
10. If excluded, preserve the record for FEC complaint, public-benefit review, contract review, or litigation analysis, while avoiding claims that require proof the current record does not support.

## Track 2: Getting Onto The Presidential Ballot

1. For each state and the District of Columbia, identify the official election office, current candidate guide, current forms, governing statutes, regulations, administrative rules, and any emergency or court-ordered changes.
2. Choose the available route: major-party nominee, recognized minor-party nominee, new-party candidate, independent candidate, elector-slate petition, write-in candidate, or substitution/withdrawal route.
3. Verify the filing calendar: declaration date, petition circulation opening date, petition submission deadline, elector deadline, vice-presidential consent deadline, fee deadline, cure deadline, challenge deadline, certification date, withdrawal deadline, ballot-printing date, and litigation cutoff.
4. Verify required papers: candidate declaration, vice-presidential consent, party certificate, elector slate, elector consent, petition sheets, circulator affidavits, notarizations, filing fee, campaign-finance forms, and ballot-name forms.
5. Verify petition mechanics: required signature count, valid-signature buffer, signer eligibility, distribution rules, circulator eligibility, circulator residency, witness rules, page format, county sorting, duplicate rules, date rules, notary rules, and preapproval options.
6. Collect signatures with a buffer tailored to the state's rejection rate and challenge environment.
7. Run internal validation before filing: voter-registration match, duplicate review, county sorting, circulator compliance, notary check, page-number audit, elector-slate consistency, and candidate-name consistency.
8. File in the required location, format, and delivery method; obtain stamped receipts and preserve copies.
9. Track verification, deficiency notices, cure windows, objections, administrative hearings, court challenges, and appeal deadlines.
10. Obtain final proof of ballot status: certification letter, official candidate list, ballot proof, court order, or official election-office confirmation.
11. Update the national ballot-access matrix and debate proof packet every time a jurisdiction changes status.

## Reform Lessons For ELEC-013

1. Debate rules can outsource gatekeeping to state ballot-access law. A neutral-looking debate criterion can incorporate fifty-one different administrative burdens.
2. Certified access matters more than claimed access. Candidates can collect or submit signatures before officials verify them, but debate sponsors may count only confirmed ballot status.
3. Timing can be outcome-determinative. A debate scheduled before some states certify candidates can favor nominees who receive party-line access over independent or minor-party candidates who rely on petition verification.
4. Polling thresholds can become downstream effects of ballot and debate visibility. A candidate excluded from ballots, polls, or debates may struggle to show the same support that the criteria demand.
5. A federal remedy does not need to guarantee every candidate a debate stage. It can require transparency, objective criteria, public-benefit disclosures, alternate qualification paths, and state ballot-access notice-and-cure floors where Congress has a defensible hook.
6. A useful first draft should pair debate-sponsor transparency with a state-by-state ballot-access publication floor: each jurisdiction should publish clear deadlines, forms, elector requirements, signature rules, cure windows, challenge procedures, and certification status in a consistent public format.
