# DOM-005 — Audit History

This file preserves the full audit history for [DOM-005](DOM-005.md). The issue page keeps only the compact Proposal Scoring summary.

## Audit History

### 2026-07-13 — Initial statutory draft and remedy-scope clarification

**Audit status:** First statutory draft complete; T1 audit pending

**Proposal-quality score:** 0

**Audit scope:** Change Audit accompanying the first legislative draft and the user's clarification that DOM-005 is limited to access to evidence and an independent State investigation.

**Statutory fit reviewed:** Section 3 of the Death in Custody Reporting Act of 2013; 34 U.S.C. § 60105; 5 U.S.C. §§ 301 and 552a(b)(7); 28 U.S.C. §§ 1442 and 1657; 44 U.S.C. § 3301; and the current organization of subtitle VI of title 34.

**Material findings:** One amendatory bill can contain the complete remedy, but the existing federal death-reporting provision is too narrow to serve as the sole container. It requires retrospective reporting to the Attorney General and does not create preservation, State access, withholding-review, or enforcement duties. The first draft therefore adds a self-contained chapter 607 to title 34, preserves the Death in Custody Reporting Act as the reporting anchor, and makes narrow conforming amendments to the Privacy Act, Federal expedition law, and Federal-officer removal law.

The scope clarification materially narrows the remedy. The [Federal Critical-Incident Evidence Access Act](../../../legislation/DOM-005.md) governs confidential officer identification, automatic preservation of evidence that exists, secure State investigative access, independent forensic participation, narrow pre-deadline withholding review, anti-circumvention, and remedies for late compliance. It does not require creation of body-camera or other recordings, create a public-disclosure right, authorize State prosecution, create State jurisdiction, decide the legality of force, or alter Supremacy Clause immunity.

The draft applies prospectively to retained evidence from covered incidents occurring during the preceding 5 years when a State investigation remains open or may lawfully be reopened. This creates a new post-effective-date access duty without imposing liability for pre-enactment withholding. Local Federal district-court jurisdiction is retained because the incident, evidence, witnesses, and State investigative authority are local and because the remedy requires hours- and days-scale relief.

**Draft elements completed:** Definitions; automatic notice and preservation; confidential officer identification; tamper-evident inventory; State activation; scene and forensic access; fixed deadlines; secure-use restrictions; exclusive withholding grounds; pre-deadline sealed judicial applications; de novo review; segregation and substitution; no automatic stay; sovereign-immunity waiver; expedition; reimbursement and fee remedies; anti-circumvention; express immunity, jurisdiction, prosecution, recording, and public-access savings clauses; oversight; appropriations authorization; and treatment of existing incidents.

**Unresolved findings:** T1 must test whether chapter 607 remains an available title 34 designation; whether the 6-hour, 12-hour, 24-hour, 72-hour, and 5-day deadlines are operationally sound; whether all covered officer and joint-operation categories are captured; how tribal investigative authority should be treated; whether the compelled-statement and Federal grand-jury provisions are sufficiently precise; how classified-information review should operate; whether reimbursement judgments require a specific Judgment Fund or agency-appropriation clause; and whether the 5-year transition period is appropriate. A full primary-source, cost, and adoption review remains outstanding.

**Score and rebaseline effect:** No formula-based score or rebaseline change. The issue remains a fixed-zero candidate until the new bill receives a T1 audit.

**Next audit need:** Conduct T1 legal-durability and implementation review of the first draft, then correct its coverage, procedure, fiscal, and intergovernmental mechanics before assigning any formula-based score.

### 2026-07-13 — Issue expansion and source-development framing

**Audit status:** Candidate expanded; no proposal-quality score assigned

**Proposal-quality score:** 0

**Audit scope:** Existing-issue disposition and candidate development after reviewing whether state access to evidence following a federal officer's use of deadly force should be placed in DOM-005, FED-004, DOM-008, DOJ-007, RIGHTS-004, or a new issue.

**Source record reviewed:** DOM, FED, DOJ, RIGHTS, REC, and CIV area boundaries; the DOM-009 candidate model; *In re Neagle*; 28 U.S.C. § 1442; *Goodyear Atomic Corp. v. Miller*; *Trump v. Vance*; 42 U.S.C. § 6961; 5 U.S.C. § 552a(b)(7); the federal and state Death in Custody Reporting Act provisions; Maine deadly-force investigation and justification statutes; Associated Press reporting on the July 13, 2026 Maine shooting and delayed or denied state access in Minnesota and Houston; Minnesota BCA statements concerning the Good, Sosa-Celis, and Pretti investigations; the Pretti evidence-preservation order; Hennepin County's evidence-access and Touhy statements; the Minnesota Attorney General's D.D.C. litigation tracker; Governor Walz's Operation Metro Surge documentation order; Associated Press and Texas Tribune reporting on the Martinez and Salgado Araujo shootings in Texas; the Texas Rangers' eventual Martinez evidence release; and the March 24, 2026 congressional request for a DHS Inspector General investigation.

**Material findings:** DOM-005 is the best existing home because its original concern—unidentified or inadequately accountable federal forces—already encompasses officer identification, incident recording, evidence preservation, state investigative access, and post-force accountability. FED-004 is a secondary federalism cross-reference. DOM-008 supplies a general expedited-review concern but cannot replace an incident-specific remedy. DOJ-007 is limited to presidential and senior executive conflict. RIGHTS-004 is immigration-specific, while the identified weakness applies across federal law-enforcement agencies.

The central legal finding is that evidence access is necessary before Supremacy Clause immunity can be meaningfully tested. The proposed reform should preserve federal-officer removal and judicial adjudication of immunity while preventing the executive branch from making the defense practically self-executing through exclusive control of the underlying evidence.

The Minnesota chronology materially strengthens the issue-admission basis. Official state sources document initial agreement to a joint investigation followed by exclusion; lack of access to scene evidence, case materials, interviews, officer identity, and body-camera information; denial of scene access despite a state warrant; a federal preservation order; a formal FBI refusal; continued Touhy disputes; and eventual production after roughly six months. The record supports describing prolonged federal withholding from state investigators, but not yet alleging destruction, falsification, criminal obstruction, or the merits of the uses of force.

The eventual Minnesota production does not resolve why federal officials changed position and should not be characterized as proven strategic conduct. It does expose a remedy-design vulnerability: an agency can retain exclusive access throughout the most important investigative period, assess the evidence and litigation risk, and then produce before a court adjudicates the legality of withholding. DOM-005 therefore treats timeliness as substantive, makes covered duties incident-triggered rather than merits-dependent, requires pre-deadline judicial applications for disputed withholding, and preserves redressable consequences after late production so that compliance cannot automatically eliminate every live controversy.

The Texas comparison adds an important design finding. The Martinez record demonstrates that a state-led investigation and state grand-jury presentation can occur, while also revealing delayed identification of federal involvement and incomplete federal recording. The Salgado Araujo record demonstrates that federal officials can decline to invite the territorial prosecutor, withhold officer identities, and retain control of evidence in the next shooting. The remedy should therefore make incident duties automatic and uniform rather than depend on discretionary federal cooperation. Neither record presently supports a project finding that evidence was destroyed, that the grand-jury presentation was defective, or that either shooting was unlawful.

**Defects corrected:** Expanded and retitled DOM-005; created a standalone issue page; added a concise issue snapshot, current manifestations, resulting damage, existing-law gaps, the immunity-testing rationale, preliminary remedy architecture, adjacent-proposal boundaries, preliminary budget considerations, neutral framing, and source-development questions; separated the South Padre Island and Houston records to show why state access cannot depend on discretionary federal cooperation; and added anti-strategic-delay, pre-deadline review, duplicate-custody, anti-circumvention, and post-production-remedy design requirements.

**Unresolved findings:** The issue lacks complete statutory text; a comprehensive primary-source survey of federal critical-incident, body-camera, evidence, privacy, privilege, grand-jury, and compelled-statement rules; the complete docket and disposition history of *Minnesota v. U.S. Department of Justice*; a comparison of the evidence eventually produced with evidence available at the time of each incident; the complete Texas Rangers Martinez file and a reliable account of what was presented to the grand jury; the developing federal and Harris County Salgado Araujo records; a fifty-state survey of investigative authority; a developed cause of action and standing analysis; specific deadlines and remedies; an appropriation estimate; prior-proposal and adoption research; and external legal or legislative-counsel review.

**Risk notes:** Drafting must not let a State direct an ongoing federal operation, obtain unrelated intelligence, evade legitimate grand-jury or compelled-statement protections, or treat evidence disclosure as proof of wrongdoing. Conversely, exceptions must not be broad enough to recreate exclusive federal control through categorical claims of investigative sensitivity, privilege, privacy, classification, or a pending federal investigation. The statute should use particularized logs, minimum-necessary redaction, secure handling, protective orders, sealed deposit, and expedited in camera review. Any post-production monetary or compliance remedy must supply concrete redress rather than purport to override Article III mootness by declaration.

**Next audit need:** Source-develop the evidence-access, privilege, immunity, recording, and enforcement design; identify the best title 5, title 28, title 34, DHS, and DOJ statutory hooks; survey prior federal body-camera and critical-incident legislation; then draft the Federal Law-Enforcement Incident Accountability Act without assigning a formula-based score until concrete legislation exists.

**Audit process feedback:** Expanding the undeveloped DOM-005 candidate was preferable to creating DOM-010 because the issue did not yet have a standalone page or a narrower adopted boundary. The expansion preserves one primary issue home and avoids separating officer identification from the evidence and procedures necessary for meaningful accountability.
