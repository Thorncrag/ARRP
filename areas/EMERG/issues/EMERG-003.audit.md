# EMERG-003 — Audit History

This file preserves the full audit history for [EMERG-003](EMERG-003.md). The issue page keeps only the compact Proposal Scoring summary.

## Audit History

### 2026-07-14 — HOR-030 integration and initial statutory draft

**Audit status:** First statutory draft complete; T1 audit pending

**Proposal-quality score:** 0

**Audit scope:** Horizon-candidate adjudication, overlap review, institutional-boundary analysis, statutory-source development, promotion of the existing EMERG-003 candidate to a standalone developed issue page, and creation of an initial legislative vehicle.

**Disposition:** HOR-030 is integrated into EMERG-003 rather than admitted as a new ICC-specific proposal. The durable defect is executive use of general emergency-economic and immigration authority to establish a major sanctions category that Congress did not specifically enact. The ICC is the principal current manifestation, not the proposal’s normative object.

**Statutory fit reviewed:** 50 U.S.C. §§ 1622 and 1701–1703; 22 U.S.C. §§ 7423 and 7433; 8 U.S.C. §§ 1182(f) and 1201(i); H.R. 23 in the 119th Congress; Executive Order 14203; the OFAC ICC-related sanctions program; and existing EMERG issue boundaries.

**Material findings:** ASPA establishes an enacted congressional baseline of substantial noncooperation and protection from unconsented ICC jurisdiction, but it does not generally authorize property blocking or entry sanctions against judges, prosecutors, lawyers, witnesses, or supporting organizations. ASPA also preserves assistance concerning foreign nationals related to Ukraine. H.R. 23 would have specifically enacted property and entry sanctions; it passed the House but did not clear Senate cloture. The failed bill is not a legal prohibition, but it confirms that the specific sanctions policy had not become law. Executive Order 14203 then used general IEEPA, NEA, and INA authority to create a functionally similar discretionary program.

The issue-admission and neutrality tests are satisfied because the proposed rule applies without regard to party, President, tribunal, country, or proceeding. It allows Congress to enact the same sanctions policy and preserves executive action against independently sanctionable conduct. It does not advocate ICC membership, recognition, cooperation, funding, surrender, or any case outcome.

**Overlap findings:** EMERG-003 is the correct existing home for the substantive authorization defect. EMERG-005 owns congressional termination, EMERG-006 owns indefinite renewal, EMERG-007 owns general findings sufficiency, and EMERG-008 owns general judicial review. HOR-026 concerns withdrawal from congressionally or Senate-approved international commitments and does not own pressure against an institution the United States never joined. JUD-011 concerns nullification of enacted congressional mandates and cannot cure the antecedent breadth of the delegation.

**Draft elements completed:** Direct-and-substantial-nexus requirement; specific-congressional-authorization rule for covered adjudicative activity; anti-circumvention protection for related legal and support activity; independent-misconduct exception; individualized findings and public reporting; conforming limits on entry suspension and visa revocation; D.D.C. cause of action and D.C. Circuit appeal; 60-day transition for existing restrictions; ASPA and ICC-policy savings clauses; implementation from otherwise available appropriations; and severability.

**Unresolved findings:** T1 must test whether the direct-nexus standard is sufficiently determinate; whether “substantial basis” and “covered adjudicative activity” capture the intended conduct without immunizing corruption or obstruction; whether the INA amendments correctly reach all entry and visa pathways used by Executive Order 14203; whether D.D.C. review should be exclusive; how classified evidence and administrative-record completion should operate; whether review standards should differ for property blocking and entry restrictions; whether transition relief improperly disturbs final penalties or third-party rights; whether the legislation should amend 50 U.S.C. § 1701 rather than § 1702; and whether implementation requires a specific authorization of appropriations.

**Score and rebaseline effect:** No formula-based score or rebaseline change. EMERG-003 remains a fixed-zero candidate until the new vehicle receives a T1 audit.

**Next audit need:** Conduct a T1 legal-durability, statutory-fit, due-process, foreign-affairs, sanctions-administration, immigration, reviewability, and implementation audit; correct the first draft before assigning any formula-based Proposal Quality Score.

**Audit process feedback:** The user’s policy/institution distinction controlled the disposition. General U.S. opposition to ICC jurisdiction can be a political choice outside ARRP, while executive creation of a sanctions regime through a broad emergency delegation presents a repairable interbranch defect. Preserving that distinction prevents the project from converting a contested international-law position into an institutional reform mandate.
