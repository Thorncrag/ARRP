# JUD-009 — Appointer-President Recusal

> ## Issue Snapshot
> **Problem:** Appointer-president cases create legitimacy risks.<br />
> **Repair:** Require tiered appointer-president recusal.<br />
> **Vehicle:** Recusal statute ([draft](../../../legislation/JUD-009.md)).
>

## Institutional Anomaly

Federal recusal law relies principally on general appearance-of-impartiality standards and judge-specific self-recusal decisions. It does not provide a categorical or tiered reassignment rule when a federal judge is assigned to a proceeding involving the president who appointed that judge.

The institutional problem is not proof of actual bias. The problem is the appearance and legitimacy risk created when a lifetime judicial appointee adjudicates the legal authority, obligations, liability, immunity, privilege, election-related status, or official conduct of the president who selected that judge for federal judicial office.

## Manifestations of the Failure

### Appointer-president as personal party

A federal judge may be assigned to a civil or criminal case where the president who appointed the judge is personally a party, personally exposed, or seeking personal legal relief. Existing law may permit recusal, but does not create a clear categorical reassignment rule.

### Appointer-president as official-capacity party

A case may name the appointing president in official capacity while materially concerning that president's own constitutional or statutory authority, immunity, privilege, records duties, emergency powers, removal authority, election-related powers, or compliance with legal limits. These cases may be formally official-capacity suits but institutionally implicate the appointing president directly.

### Broader official-capacity cases

Some official-capacity suits name the president even where relief may be available against the United States, an agency, or a subordinate officer. A broad recusal rule may be justified for public legitimacy, but it also creates judge-shopping risks if parties can trigger recusal merely by naming the president unnecessarily.

## Concrete Manifestations and Examples

### United States v. Trump classified-documents prosecution — appointer-president as criminal defendant

Judge Aileen Cannon, appointed to the federal bench by President Donald Trump, was assigned to preside over the federal classified-documents criminal case against Trump after his 2023 indictment. The assignment was reported as random. Public calls for recusal followed, based on the appearance-of-impartiality concern created by a Trump appointee presiding over a criminal prosecution of the president who appointed her and by her earlier rulings in a related Mar-a-Lago civil matter.

The point for JUD-009 is not to adjudicate whether Judge Cannon was actually biased or whether any particular ruling was correct. The point is that existing recusal law left a nationally consequential criminal prosecution of an appointing president to ordinary self-recusal standards rather than a categorical or tiered reassignment rule.

**Failure illustrated:** appointer-president as personal criminal defendant; appearance-of-impartiality controversy; discretionary self-recusal in a high-stakes presidential case.

**Reform connection:** supports Tier 1 mandatory recusal where the appointing president is a criminal defendant or personally exposed.

### Trump v. United States / Mar-a-Lago special-master litigation — appointer-president seeking personal legal relief

Before the classified-documents indictment, Judge Cannon presided over civil litigation brought by Trump seeking appointment of a special master and restrictions on government use of materials seized at Mar-a-Lago. The Eleventh Circuit later reversed key orders and concluded that the district court had improperly exercised jurisdiction.

Again, the issue is not whether the appointing president's appointee was actually biased. The institutional example is that a judge appointed by the litigating former president was assigned to adjudicate personal relief sought by that former president in a matter closely connected to possible criminal exposure.

**Failure illustrated:** appointer-president as personal litigant seeking individualized relief; later legitimacy controversy amplified by appellate reversal.

**Reform connection:** supports Tier 1 mandatory recusal for cases where the appointing president is an individual-capacity party or seeks personal legal relief.

### Special-counsel report litigation — appointer-president and post-dismissal accountability materials

After dismissal of the classified-documents prosecution, litigation continued over access to and release of special-counsel report materials concerning the classified-documents matter. Judge Cannon reportedly blocked release or sharing of the classified-documents volume outside the Department of Justice, producing further public controversy over whether a judge appointed by Trump should continue deciding matters connected to the investigation and prosecution of Trump.

This example illustrates that appointer-president recusal questions may persist after dismissal of a criminal case, including disputes over records, reports, transparency, congressional access, and post-case accountability materials.

**Failure illustrated:** continuing appointer-president-adjacent proceedings after dismissal; public legitimacy concern over records and accountability materials.

**Reform connection:** supports extending Tier 1 or Tier 2 recusal to proceedings materially connected to the appointing president's personal criminal exposure, records obligations, or post-case accountability disputes.

### Official-capacity presidential power litigation — president as named official-capacity party

Presidents are often named in suits challenging executive orders, emergency powers, removal decisions, records obligations, privilege assertions, immigration directives, national-security measures, or other exercises of presidential authority. Where the assigned judge was appointed by that same president, the case may formally be an official-capacity matter while substantively requiring adjudication of the appointing president's own legal authority or compliance with legal limits.

**Failure illustrated:** formal official-capacity posture may obscure direct institutional stakes for the appointing president.

**Reform connection:** supports Tier 2 mandatory recusal for official-capacity cases materially concerning the appointing president's own constitutional or statutory powers, immunity, privilege, records duties, emergency authority, removal authority, election-related status, or compliance with judicial or statutory limits.

### Routine agency litigation with president named unnecessarily — judge-shopping risk

Some cases name the president as a defendant even though complete relief may be available against the United States, an agency, or a subordinate officer. A broad automatic recusal rule could allow parties to trigger reassignment simply by naming the president unnecessarily.

**Failure illustrated:** overbroad recusal rules can invite strategic pleading and judge-shopping.

**Reform connection:** supports Tier 3 presumptive, rebuttable recusal for broader official-capacity cases and an anti-gamesmanship provision.

## Resulting Damage

Absence of a clear rule can undermine public confidence, encourage strategic recusal motions, leave high-stakes presidential cases dependent on discretionary self-recusal, and cause later legitimacy disputes even where the judge acts in good faith and applies law correctly.

## Underlying Weakness

Existing law may lack:

- categorical mandatory recusal where the appointing president is a personal party;
- mandatory recusal for official-capacity cases materially involving the appointing president's own powers, duties, immunity, privilege, records obligations, emergency authority, removal authority, election-related status, or compliance with legal limits;
- a rebuttable presumption for broader official-capacity cases;
- an anti-gamesmanship rule preventing strategic naming of the president solely to force reassignment;
- a neutral reassignment procedure; and
- a severability structure preserving narrower recusal tiers if broader tiers are held invalid.

## Repair and Prevention

Congress should amend 28 U.S.C. § 455 or enact a related federal recusal provision establishing a tiered appointer-president recusal rule:

1. **Tier 1 — mandatory recusal for personal-interest cases.** A judge must recuse where the president who nominated the judge is a party in an individual capacity, a criminal defendant, or has a direct personal, financial, electoral, reputational, civil, or criminal legal interest.
2. **Tier 2 — mandatory recusal for core official-authority cases.** A judge must recuse where the appointing president is a party in official capacity and the proceeding materially concerns that president's own constitutional or statutory powers, duties, immunity, privilege, records obligations, emergency authority, removal authority, election-related status, or compliance with judicial or statutory limits.
3. **Tier 3 — presumptive recusal for broader official-capacity cases.** A judge is presumptively recused where the appointing president is a named official-capacity party, subject to rebuttal where the case is routine, agency-focused, does not materially implicate the president's own legal authority or interests, and complete relief is available without the president as a necessary party.

The legislation should include anti-gamesmanship language and a robust severability clause so that if broader official-capacity coverage is held invalid or overbroad, narrower personal-interest and core-authority recusal rules remain enforceable.

## Proposal Survey

Federal recusal law already requires disqualification where impartiality might reasonably be questioned or where specified personal interests exist. See [28 U.S.C. § 455](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/28/455). Existing law, however, relies heavily on judge-specific assessment and does not create an appointer-president category.

Other models include random assignment, reassignment after appellate reversal, and specialized recusal rules for financial or familial interests. JUD-009 extends the categorical-recusal model to the appointer-president relationship while including anti-gamesmanship limits for routine official-capacity cases.

## Least-Complex Adequate Remedy

The least-complex adequate remedy is a targeted amendment to federal recusal law. The remedy should not attempt to adjudicate whether any particular judge acted improperly in any past case. It should create prospective, neutral, administrable reassignment rules for future cases.

## Annotation

**Neutrality.** The rule applies regardless of party, president, appointing administration, ideology, judge, litigant, or expected outcome.

**No Finding of Actual Bias.** The issue is institutional legitimacy and administrability, not proof that an appointee judge is actually biased.

**Examples Not Findings.** Concrete examples, including Judge Cannon's assignment in Trump-related matters, are included as manifestations of appearance and legitimacy concerns, not as findings of misconduct, bias, or legal error beyond what was determined by reviewing courts.

**Severability.** The tiered structure is intentional. If a court rejects broad official-capacity coverage, narrower personal-party and core-authority coverage should remain in force.
