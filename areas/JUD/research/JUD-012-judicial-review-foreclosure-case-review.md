# JUD-012 Judicial Review Foreclosure Case Review

This internal source-development memorandum records the review that supported admission of HOR-036 as JUD-012. The inquiry is whether legally consequential federal action can remain without judicial adjudication of its legality because no viable cause of action, plaintiff, evidence procedure, or review route exists. It does not assume that unadjudicated allegations are true, that challenged action was unlawful, or that every threshold dismissal identifies an institutional defect.

## Finding

The original six-case sample did not establish the proposed failure because it included ordinary merits decisions, preliminary-relief denials, and dismissals preserving later review. The expanded review identifies a defensible cross-subject record involving unavailable constitutional remedies, state-secrets dismissals, statutory review bars, and limits on review of presidential compliance. That record supports issue admission, but not immediate selection of one general remedy.

Additional supporting cases should be sought before legislation is drafted. The next pass should test whether the demonstrated effects extend beyond national-security secrecy, immigration, and a small number of unusual remedial settings; whether other administrations and subject areas supply comparable examples; and whether subject-specific remedies already cure important categories.

## Qualification Test

A case supports JUD-012 only when:

1. a concrete and legally consequential federal action is identified;
2. a nonfrivolous challenge alleges that the action violated a constitutional, statutory, or procedural limit;
3. a threshold or remedial barrier prevents adjudication of the underlying legality;
4. no realistic substitute plaintiff, claim, forum, later proceeding, or independent review mechanism remains; and
5. the action is completed or produces consequences that prospective review cannot adequately test or repair.

Political-question decisions, ordinary merits losses, preliminary-relief denials, correctable pleading errors, and dismissals preserving realistic later review are excluded. A court may review the privilege, jurisdictional doctrine, or cause-of-action question without deciding the legality of the underlying action; that still may satisfy the test.

## Strong Supporting Manifestations

### Unavailable federal constitutional remedies

- ***Tun-Cos v. Perrotte*.** Nine men alleged that ICE officers stopped and detained them without reasonable suspicion, entered homes without warrants or consent, and seized them unlawfully. The Fourth Circuit [declined to recognize a *Bivens* action](https://www.ca4.uscourts.gov/opinions/181451.P.pdf) and acknowledged that immigration procedures often do not redress constitutional violations occurring outside removal proceedings. The completed conduct could not be tested through later removal review.

- ***Meshal v. Higgenbotham*.** A citizen alleged that federal agents detained him abroad for months without charges or judicial process and subjected him to coercive interrogation and transfer. The parties agreed that he had no alternative remedy, but the D.C. Circuit [declined to recognize a damages action](https://media.cadc.uscourts.gov/opinions/docs/2015/10/14-5194.pdf) because of national-security and extraterritoriality concerns.

- ***Hernández v. Mesa*.** A Border Patrol agent standing in the United States shot and killed a teenager across the border. DOJ declined prosecution, the United States denied Mexico's extradition request, and the Supreme Court [declined to extend *Bivens*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/19pdf/17-1678_m6io.pdf). The Court did not decide the underlying constitutional question. DOM-005 retains the federal critical-incident and evidence-access cross-reference.

### State-secrets foreclosure

- ***El-Masri v. United States*.** The plaintiff alleged CIA abduction, extraordinary rendition, detention, and abuse. After the government asserted the state-secrets privilege, the Fourth Circuit [affirmed dismissal](https://www.ca4.uscourts.gov/Opinions/Published/061667.P.pdf) because the litigation could not proceed without protected information.

- ***Mohamed v. Jeppesen Dataplan, Inc.*.** Rendition victims sued a contractor alleged to have provided logistical support. The United States intervened, and the en banc Ninth Circuit [affirmed dismissal](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2010/09/08/08-15693.pdf) because there was no feasible way to litigate liability without an unacceptable disclosure risk.

- ***Wikimedia Foundation v. National Security Agency*.** The Fourth Circuit found a genuine factual dispute relevant to standing but held that [state-secrets protection prevented further litigation](https://www.ca4.uscourts.gov/opinions/201191.P.pdf) of the challenged surveillance.

- ***Abilt v. Central Intelligence Agency*.** A covert employee alleged disability discrimination and retaliation. The Fourth Circuit [affirmed dismissal](https://www.ca4.uscourts.gov/opinions/published/152568.P.pdf) because privileged information was necessary to litigate both the employee's claims and the agency's defenses. This supplies a nonpartisan employment-law example outside rendition and bulk surveillance.

The Congressional Research Service's [state-secrets doctrine report](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47081) supplies the legislative and doctrinal map for this category; publication-ready propositions remain anchored in the controlling opinions above.

### Statutory and structural review barriers

- ***Mullin v. Doe / Trump v. Miot*.** The Supreme Court held that [8 U.S.C. § 1254a(b)(5)(A) bars nonconstitutional challenges](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/25pdf/25-1083_f204.pdf) to TPS terminations, including alleged failures to follow mandatory procedures. Constitutional claims remain reviewable, making this a claim-specific rather than total gap. RIGHTS-002 remains the primary substantive home.

- ***Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington v. Trump*.** Plaintiffs alleged that White House personnel used message-deleting applications for official business. The D.C. Circuit held that [courts generally cannot supervise day-to-day presidential compliance](https://media.cadc.uscourts.gov/opinions/docs/2019/05/18-5150.pdf) with the Presidential Records Act. REC-001 remains the primary records-integrity home.

- **Congressional Review Act submission duties.** The D.C. Circuit held in [*Montanans for Multiple Use v. Barbouletos*](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/cadc/08-5131/08-5131-1184004-2011-03-24.html) that 5 U.S.C. § 805 prevented judicial enforcement of an alleged failure to submit a covered rule to Congress. This is a clean example of an express congressional process duty that may lack a judicial enforcement route, although later review must assess the circuit split and availability of other challenges to the underlying rule.

- ***Thryv, Inc. v. Click-to-Call Technologies, LP*.** The Patent and Trademark Office instituted inter partes review despite an alleged statutory time bar and later canceled patent claims. The Supreme Court [held the institution decision unreviewable](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/19pdf/18-916diff_3c4j.pdf) under the specialized statute. Courts could review patentability, but not whether the agency lawfully initiated the proceeding.

- ***United States v. Fausto*.** A federal employee was excluded from the Civil Service Reform Act's review system, and the Supreme Court [held that the statutory scheme also foreclosed alternative review](https://www.govinfo.gov/app/details/USREPORTS-484/USREPORTS-484-439/context). Congress later repaired much of that exclusion through the Civil Service Due Process Amendments of 1990. CIV-001 remains the substantive cross-route.

## Supporting but Boundary-Sensitive Examples

- ***Public Citizen v. Trump* and *California v. Trump*.** Organizations, affected members, and States challenged Executive Order 13,771's regulatory offset regime but could not establish which delayed or abandoned rules were caused by the order. The lack of a government mechanism identifying affected rules made facial standing unusually difficult. Specific later regulatory actions sometimes remained reviewable, so these cases support the government-controlled-causation problem but are not the principal complete-foreclosure examples.

- ***Dalton v. Specter*.** The Supreme Court held that subordinate recommendations in a military-base-closure process were not final agency action and that the President was not an APA agency. The statutory-compliance challenge therefore failed. Because Congress deliberately created a Commission, presidential approval, and congressional disapproval process and the House rejected disapproval, the case is a doctrinal analogue and boundary example rather than proof that the political design itself was defective.

- ***Patchak v. Zinke*.** Congress required dismissal of pending and future litigation concerning a particular land-trust decision while ratifying the underlying acquisition. The case demonstrates targeted jurisdiction stripping, but Congress's substantive ratification makes it an awkward example of executive action escaping review.

- ***FBI v. Fazaga*.** The Supreme Court held that a FISA provision did not displace the state-secrets privilege but did not decide whether the privilege applied or whether dismissal was proper. Later proceedings continued. Treat as monitoring rather than completed foreclosure.

## Rejected Comparators and Original-Sample Correction

- ***[Gwich'in Steering Committee v. Bernhardt](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/alaska/akdce/3%3A2020cv00204/65089/69/).*** Preliminary relief was denied because the challenged decision authorized no immediate on-the-ground activity and the court expected to reach the merits before immediate physical injury. This was an interim-relief decision, not review foreclosure.

- ***California v. Wheeler*, D.C. Circuit methane-rule litigation.** The court denied interim relief and expedited the merits schedule; Congress later [disapproved the rule](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/senate-joint-resolution/14). This did not eliminate a merits route.

- ***Ramos v. Wolf*.** The vacated panel opinion raised the TPS review-bar issue, but the current controlling source is *Mullin*.

- ***[Wild Virginia v. Council on Environmental Quality](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca4/21-1839/21-1839-2022-12-22.html).*** The court preserved application-specific challenges. It illustrates the cost of delayed review, not the absence of a later route.

- ***[California v. Wheeler](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/california/candce/3%3A2020cv03005/358957/171/)*,* Navigable Waters Protection Rule litigation.** The court denied preliminary relief after applying the ordinary factors; later proceedings and a separate vacatur followed.

- ***[Northern Alaska Environmental Center v. Department of the Interior](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/19-35008/19-35008-2020-07-09.html).*** The court decided the challenged lease-sale analysis on the merits while treating one contention as waived.

- ***Clapper v. Amnesty International USA*.** The plaintiffs lacked standing, but the Court identified other potential review routes involving surveillance evidence and affected service providers.

- ***United States v. Texas*.** The standing rule concerned traditional executive enforcement discretion and is too close to the political-policy boundary to support JUD-012.

- ***Egbert v. Boule*.** The Court treated the Border Patrol grievance process as an alternative remedial mechanism. It demonstrates restrictive *Bivens* doctrine but is weaker under the no-realistic-substitute requirement.

- ***Buchanan v. Barr* and ***Ziglar v. Abbasi*.** Other forms of equitable or habeas review remained available. They demonstrate remedy limitations rather than complete review foreclosure.

- **Political-question decisions.** *Rucho v. Common Cause*, *El-Shifa Pharmaceutical Industries v. United States*, *Al-Aulaqi v. Obama*, and comparable decisions are outside JUD-012's chosen scope.

## Historical Repairs and Legislative Models

Congress has repeatedly altered review architecture without purporting to override Article III:

- the 1976 amendment to [5 U.S.C. § 702](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/STATUTE-90/pdf/STATUTE-90-Pg2721.pdf) removed sovereign immunity as a general barrier to nonmonetary APA relief;
- the [Veterans' Judicial Review Act of 1988](https://www.congress.gov/100/statute/STATUTE-102/STATUTE-102-Pg4105.pdf) created independent judicial review of veterans-benefit decisions after decades without a court appeal;
- the Civil Service Due Process Amendments of 1990 repaired much of the *Fausto* employee-review exclusion;
- prior State Secrets Protection Acts proposed uniform judicial procedures, in-camera review, substitutions, and limits on dismissal; and
- the [Bivens Act of 2025](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/6091/text) proposes a statutory civil remedy against persons acting under federal authority.

These models establish legislative capacity to repair particular review gaps. They do not yet establish that one statute can coherently govern every JUD-012 mechanism.

## Recommendation and Next Research

The expanded record supports independent admission in A-04 as **JUD-012 — Government Action Insulated from Judicial Review**. JUD-012 should own the absence of an effective legality-review route; subject-specific proposals should retain the underlying rights, records duties, enforcement conduct, regulatory authority, and policy questions.

Before drafting, conduct an additional supporting-case search organized by mechanism and subject area:

1. standing failures caused by government-controlled causation or secrecy;
2. absent statutory causes of action for completed constitutional injury;
3. sovereign-immunity or officer-suit gaps with no APA, Tucker Act, habeas, or other substitute;
4. statutory review bars that make mandatory duties unenforceable;
5. final presidential action implementing statute outside APA review;
6. privilege rules that require whole-case dismissal despite secure-procedure alternatives;
7. specialized review schemes that exclude a class of claims or claimants; and
8. historical examples in which Congress repaired a comparable gap.

For every added case, record the action, challenged legal duty, exact barrier, available alternatives, resulting harm, primary subject route, and whether the likely remedy is general or subject-specific. If the expanded record remains concentrated in a few exceptional fields or requires incompatible remedies, JUD-012 should narrow or become a coordinated umbrella rather than a universal judicial-review act.
