### 2026-07-13 — T2 development audit

**Audit status:** T2 development audit complete; T3 readiness audit pending.

**Proposal-quality score:** 70 / 100 (Developed Draft).

**Scope:** Cumulative review of the T1 record, issue page, complete Interbranch Review Framework Act, governing methodology and anti-nullification boundaries, Article III plaintiff categories, express cause of action and jurisdiction, sovereign-immunity waiver, presidential and subordinate-officer relief, title 28 assignment and three-judge provisions, remedial scope, functional analogues, current and historical congressional proposals, D.D.C. capacity, cost comparators, adoption record, current Supreme Court decisions, and issue-to-legislation alignment.

**Current-status, mootness, and pending-judicial-vulnerability finding:** No current statute or controlling decision makes the proposal moot. *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* and *Trump v. Slaughter* preserve the central distinction between enforcing enacted limits and intruding on constitutionally protected executive authority. *Trump v. CASA, Inc.* materially constrains remedial scope by tying injunctions to complete relief for plaintiffs with standing. Recent standing decisions, including *FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine* and *Murthy v. Missouri*, confirm that Congress cannot substitute eligible-plaintiff labels for concrete injury, causation, redressability, and forward-looking injury. T3 must refresh pending Supreme Court and D.C. Circuit merits matters involving impoundment, agency dismantling, officer removal, reviewability, and executive compliance immediately before circulation.

**Standing, jurisdiction, and cause-of-action finding:** The general public, ideological plaintiffs, and individual legislators with only generalized institutional objections remain outside the bill. *U.S. House of Representatives v. Mnuchin* supports standing for a chamber alleging specific impairment of its appropriations power, and *Committee on the Judiciary v. McGahn* supports an authorized committee alleging a concrete informational injury; both reinforce that authorization and injury are distinct from an express cause of action. Section 2303 supplies that cause, section 2302(g) supplies original and exclusive D.D.C. jurisdiction, and section 2303(d) preserves every Article III and justiciability requirement. The eligible categories are defensible as routing categories only because they do not purport to manufacture standing.

**Sovereign-immunity and defendants finding:** The non-damages waiver tracks the core structure of 5 U.S.C. § 702 but extends to the United States, agencies, and officials covered by JUD-011's independent cause. T2 revised the bill to permit judgment against the United States and require mandatory or injunctive decrees to identify responsible officers by name or title and bind successors to the extent permitted by law. Relief against the President personally remains exceptional and subordinate-officer relief is preferred. T3 should verify the final text against *Franklin v. Massachusetts*, *Mississippi v. Johnson*, *Swan v. Clinton*, nonstatutory ultra vires review, mandamus, and any statute-specific exclusive-review provisions.

**Judicial-assignment and panel finding:** Sections 291 and 292 ordinarily use request, public-interest, or certificate mechanisms; section 295 requires source-circuit consent for active judges and public filing of assignments; section 296 supplies assigned judges' powers. Section 2284 authorizes Congress to require a three-judge district court but uses the circuit chief for ordinary designations. FISA, the alien-terrorist removal court, and the Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation demonstrate that Congress may create national Article III rosters or panels with direct statutory designation rules. T2 revised section 2304 to make JUD-011 selections directly authorized rather than dependent on a case-specific request or certificate while preserving section 295 consent and section 296 powers where consistent. Legislative counsel and AOUSC should still test roster terms, senior-judge service, chief-justice duties, random assignment, recusals, replacement, and the modification of section 2284.

**Relief and procedure finding:** The bill preserves Rule 65-type interim-relief findings, due process, protected-information law, issuing-court authority, habeas and criminal-process boundaries, and supplemental-jurisdiction and transfer limits. T2 added a *CASA*-responsive provision limiting injunctions to complete relief for each plaintiff with standing while preserving certified-class and indivisible-injury relief. The Act expressly displaces section 1253 in favor of D.C. Circuit review. T3 must conduct a rule-by-rule check of Rules 12, 23, 24, 41, 42, 53, 65, and 71; sections 1291, 1292, 1404, 1406, 1631, 1651, and 1657; appellate stay and mandamus practice; and D.D.C. and D.C. Circuit local rules.

**Existing-law and prior-proposal finding:** Direct and functional analogues now exceed the T2 minimum. Existing 2 U.S.C. § 687 authorizes Comptroller General D.D.C. litigation to release unlawfully withheld funds. H.R. 3454 and S. 2838 are narrower current bills. The 117th-Congress H.R. 5314 package had 168 cosponsors and passed the House 220–208 before stalling in the Senate. Section 1365 and the Senate Legal Counsel statutes illustrate express congressional-plaintiff authorization but also important executive-privilege limits. Sections 2284 and 1407, 50 U.S.C. § 1803, and 8 U.S.C. § 1532 provide panel, roster, or specialized-court analogues. None combines a general anti-nullification cause, automatic subject-matter-neutral coverage, the proposed roster, protected funding, and D.C. Circuit route.

**Budget and implementation finding:** The bill proposes $10 million in startup direct spending, $15 million annually for seven years, and two D.D.C. judgeships. CBO's H.R. 1702 estimate supports ordinary judge and court-administration cost scale but does not estimate secure records, specialized staff, special masters, emergency operation, or appellate load. D.D.C.'s March 2025 profile reported 15 judgeships, 4,311 filings, 6,464 pending matters, and 309 weighted filings per judgeship. The Judicial Conference's 2025 recommendations did not request a D.D.C. seat. The proposed seats therefore require justification as contingent new-program capacity, not as correction of a demonstrated ordinary-caseload shortage. The staged second seat and three-year capacity review reduce overbuilding risk, but T3 should evaluate whether the second seat should instead be conditional on workload certification and should identify the direct-spending, PAYGO, facilities, security, and D.C. Circuit impacts more precisely.

**Adoption and opposition finding:** H.R. 5314 shows one-chamber viability for a broad executive-accountability package under unified partisan support, while the current narrower bills identify a sponsor pool. The evidence does not show bipartisan support for the general framework, Senate viability, proposal-specific public support, judiciary endorsement, or acceptance of new judgeships and protected direct spending. Adoption remains 2 / 12, friction remains 87 / 100, and staging narrower appropriations-enforcement, expedition, transparency, and capacity components remains the most evidence-bound pathway adjustment.

**Issue-to-legislation and Internal Remedy-Fit finding:** The page and bill remain aligned on no-opt-in coverage, concrete injury, a substantial-and-sustained nullification threshold, older mandates and ongoing conduct, preferred subordinate-officer relief, D.D.C. jurisdiction, D.C. Circuit appeal, shared infrastructure, and independently enactable REG-001 and FUND-001 alternatives. T2's assignment, sovereign-immunity, and remedial-scope changes improve durability without altering the governing institutional diagnosis or the preferred-remedy relationship.

**Score calculation:** Structural 8/8; Evidence 8/12; Legal Fit 7/10; Prior-Proposal 6/8; Remedy 9/12; Implementation 6/8; Abuse Resistance 7/8; Drafting 7/8; Cogency 6/6; Adoption 2/12; Project Integration 4/4; External Review 0/4; Penalties 0. Total: 70/100.

**Corrections made:** Added an express responsible-officer and successor requirement for mandatory decrees; clarified that random roster designation is directly authorized by JUD-011 while preserving section 295 consent and section 296 powers; added complete-plaintiff-relief limits responsive to *CASA*; expanded direct and functional analogue analysis; corrected fiscal presentation to distinguish direct spending from an official cost score; and completed the first full formula rebaseline.

**Unresolved findings for T3:** Complete the procedural-statute, federal-rule, local-rule, and legislative-form crosswalk; verify *Franklin*, *Johnson*, *Swan*, ultra vires, mandamus, and exclusive-review interactions; refresh pending executive-power and nonexecution cases; test whether a single judge may perform each proposed threshold and emergency function under section 2284; determine whether the second D.D.C. seat should be workload-contingent; develop opposition and reciprocity analysis; verify every source-backed factual statement; and obtain or prepare for AOUSC, legislative-counsel, CBO, and qualified constitutional-law review.

**Audit Process Feedback:** The T2 cluster method worked: legal gateway, assignment, procedure, analogues, budget, and adoption were reviewed separately so no single complex doctrine consumed the tier. The score increase reflects verified sources and three text changes, not audit repetition. JUD-011 remains below Review Ready because procedural integration, launch capacity, current merits exposure, and external validation are not complete.
