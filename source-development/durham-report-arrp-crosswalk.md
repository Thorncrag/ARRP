# Durham Report / ARRP Crosswalk

## Purpose

This crosswalk maps Special Counsel John Durham's final report to ARRP proposals and source-development priorities. The report is useful because it identifies institutional weaknesses involving politically sensitive counterintelligence and criminal investigations, FISA application accuracy, analytical rigor, confirmation bias, source vetting, politically affiliated information, internal challenge functions, and the use of special counsel mechanisms to review prior investigative decisions.

The report must be used with the same discipline that applies to indictments, the Mueller Report, and later Special Counsel reports. It is a prosecutorial report and prosecution/declination record, not an adjudicated judicial finding after trial. It may identify institutional flaws, alleged fact patterns, procedural posture, source leads, and reform ideas, but it should not be used to support the truth of an allegation unless the project verifies the specific cited evidence or corroborates the claim through admitted records, judicial findings, official records, inspector-general findings, FISC orders, sworn testimony, or other reliable sources.

## Summary Assessment

The Durham Report most directly supports source development for [DOJ-003](../areas/DOJ/issues/DOJ-003.md), because its central institutional value is not proving partisan intent but stress-testing how DOJ and FBI should handle politically sensitive investigative predicates, comparator treatment, source vetting, confirmation bias, and anomaly review.

It also supports or tests:

- [DOJ-007](../areas/DOJ/issues/DOJ-007.md), because it is a special-counsel final report reviewing a politically sensitive prior investigation and therefore helps test reporting, appointment, scope, and institutional-legitimacy design.
- [A-09 Inspectors General, Whistleblowers, and Internal Oversight](../areas/OVS/README.md), because the report repeatedly relies on, disputes, or responds to the DOJ Inspector General's Crossfire Hurricane/FISA findings and proposes internal challenge or red-team functions.
- [A-15 Congressional Oversight and Executive Accountability](../areas/CONG/README.md), because FISA, counterintelligence, and politically sensitive investigations often require classified or partially classified oversight channels.
- [A-18 Government Information, Scientific, Statistical, and Factual Integrity](../areas/FACT/README.md), where official public claims about investigations, source reliability, and institutional conclusions need careful labeling to avoid misleading public presentation.
- Possible horizon review for a dedicated issue on politically sensitive counterintelligence/FISA investigations involving presidential campaigns, parties, candidates, or transition teams.

No immediate new developed proposal is required solely because of the report. The report's strongest use is as a source-development and remedy-stress document for politically sensitive investigation controls.

## Coverage Gaps and Follow-Up Reviews

### Highest-value follow-up audits

1. **DOJ-003 politically sensitive investigation check.** Test whether DOJ-003's anomaly-review model should extend upstream from charging outcomes to investigative-opening, escalation, source-vetting, and politically affiliated information decisions.
2. **A-09 internal challenge function review.** Use the Durham red-team/internal-challenge discussion to source-develop candidate issues on internal oversight, access to contrary information, and confirmation-bias controls.
3. **FISA/counterintelligence horizon review.** Decide whether ARRP needs a distinct issue for campaign-related counterintelligence investigations, FISA applications, FISC accuracy duties, and classified oversight.
4. **DOJ-007 report-design comparison.** Compare Durham, Mueller, and Smith final-report posture for special-counsel report timing, scope, public release, redactions, classified appendices, and treatment of nonadjudicated allegations.
5. **Adverse-evidence handling pass.** Any issue using Durham should explicitly preserve the tensions among Durham, the DOJ OIG report, FISC orders, later criminal-case outcomes, congressional reports, and reliable secondary synthesis.

## Source-Use Rules

When using this report in issue pages, audits, or legislation notes:

- describe it as a Special Counsel report, prosecution/declination assessment, or source-development lead;
- do not describe its factual narrative as a final judicial finding or as independently verified fact;
- do not use the report to support the truth of an allegation unless the project verifies the specific cited evidence or corroborates the allegation through admitted records, judicial findings, official records, inspector-general findings, FISC orders, sworn testimony, or other reliable sources;
- use it for identifying institutional flaws, procedural posture, source leads, classified-appendix constraints, and remedy stress tests, with proper qualification;
- pair predication and FISA propositions with the DOJ Inspector General report, FISC orders, FISA reform materials, and court records where available;
- pair criminal-prosecution propositions with the relevant indictments, plea agreements, verdicts, judgments, sentencing records, or declination explanations;
- preserve the report's own adverse qualifications, including its statements about insufficient proof on some intent theories and uncertainty around foreign-derived intelligence;
- avoid converting disagreement between Durham and the DOJ Inspector General into a factual conclusion without explaining the source conflict.

## Working Conclusion

The Durham Report warrants retained-source and crosswalk treatment. Its value to ARRP is not that it proves a partisan conspiracy or adjudicates Crossfire Hurricane facts. Its value is that it exposes recurring institutional design questions: how DOJ and FBI should open and escalate politically sensitive investigations, how they should handle information from political actors or foreign intelligence channels, how FISA applications should present uncertainty and contrary facts, how internal challenge functions should work, and how special-counsel reports should be used without laundering nonadjudicated allegations into project fact.

The report should trigger disciplined source-development follow-up, especially for DOJ-003, DOJ-007, A-09, and a possible FISA/counterintelligence horizon issue.
